The Science Behind Breaks

Breaks can improve focus


Another 2011 study overturned a decades-old idea about the nature of attention. With 84 participants divided into four groups, each completed the same repetitive, computerized task for 50 minutes. While it was found that there is nothing more computerized and repetitive than the average college student's daily schedule, the study presented some interesting findings about the value of a break. Out of the four groups, only those who had breaks in between their tasks had a consistent performance over time.

Breaks can prevent “decision fatigue"


If you are one of the millions of Americans that struggles to haul themselves out of bed in the morning, or to get motivated to start the day, you may be surprised to learn about the functions of the prefrontal cortex. This part of the brain fights those impulses and bad habits, and works hard throughout the day to focus our goals, help us think logically, and institute willpower to override impulse. However, you may be surprised to hear that life isn’t all about working, in fact, many studies have shown a positive effect when we allow our brain to rest in between work.

Breaks allow for creative thinking


When we daydream, our brains become highly active in areas that are associated with complex problem-solving. Specifically, the lateral prefrontal cortex and the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex. We all let our mind wander, whether it be thinking about the next Tiger King episode and where the bleep Carole Baskin is at, or about your professor not wearing pants on a Zoom call. Interestingly enough, letting your mind wander on something imaginative might let you solve that big problem you’ve been working on all day, so let those Tiger King thoughts flow!
